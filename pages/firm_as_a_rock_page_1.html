Firm As A Rock
The following exposition explores new possibilities for fabrication involving embedded frameworks and lattices inside of concrete and ceramics.
When 3D printing first exploded into popular awareness when it hit consumer shelves in the early 2010's, I thought that this is it! The idea of being able to fabricate any form, even if only in plastic, was so far removed from the household reality of the 90's that I grew up in, that I was certain we were at the dawn of a new era in human history. We were finally going to shift into a future where, much like the ever mutating and progressing computer hardware of my childhood, surely new and ever-expanding possibilities were now going to be unveiled around every corner in the embodiment of some new device or upgrade.

I waved off the critiques of the cynics saying that plastic was only good for junk. While I would admit to the validity of their claims, still, this was only the beginning. Didn't they understand? Perhaps in two years full multicolor 3d prints would be the norm, the expensive process of toy making now 'democratized' as they say these days. The resolutions and speeds of these printers would likely go up year after year, the same as the ever rising television screen resolutions. And then quite soon, I thought, high quality affordable 3d printing of metal would be slowly making it's way into every so-and-so household like some fancy new washing machine feature. No, the future had arrived! The dawn of a new age was on the horizon. Surely, at last!

Well, though I hate to admit it, but man was I wrong! Now a decade-and-a-half later and I can barely see any difference from the crap the things spit out these days to what was on offer way back then. Sure, the printers are a bit faster, a bit easier to set up, and other incremental enhancements. Yes there are shelves and shelves of spools of new materials, all essentially bottom of the barrel plastics unless you're willing to pay an arm and a leg. But real improvement? Let's be honest. Meh!

More truthfully just mountains and mountains of trash added to already existing floating islands. And oversaturated junk cluttering up street market stalls. This is not the promised land!

I do want to start off however by saying that I am constantly awed by the ingenuity of creators who take these technologies and then do the best they can with them in making awesome objects and devices. And I am especially thankful to those who choose to freely share their creations and findings online in the form of downloadable files, video tutorials or even just on-point comments, which can all prove to be immensely valuable. To those who have been collectively designated as the online 'maker community', who contribute back to this pool of human knowledge and technical ability. I raise a glass to all of you!




Having said that, I have to concede that these home inventors utilising 3D printing all too quickly run into the fundamental limitations of this fabrication method and of the easily available and affordable printing materials. This especially when attempting to make more complex production machines such as power looms and lathes.



You get fast, you get accurate, you get both fast and accurate, but you can't really have any of those things with 3D printed plastic machinery. It just doesn't have the required material properties, the strength and rigidity to match steel or wood for such applications.



And people have tried. A plethora of tutorials exist on 'how to get the most out of your 3D print', but these usually still amount to only marginal improvements.






And while there are purportedly some incredible next-generation materials on the horizon, these will likely remain expensive and hard to come by for some time still. On top of that stronger plastics eventually just make for taller landfills.




And so in response to this dire situation I set out to discover new methods of home fabrication.
I pivoted quite early in my explorations to focus mainly on the idea of lattices/frameworks embedded inside of rigid materials like concrete or ceramic.
In theory such a framework could then provide tensile ductility to a material that is already strong in terms of it's compressive strength.






Since I started off this article by talking about 3d printers, I'll continue by first looking at what can be done with standard FDM 3d printing in order to print such embedded structures in plastic. And then I'll continue further by considering machines that might use metal to fabricate these embedded frameworks utilising a variety of approaches.

While searching the web to see if anyone has attempted this before, I promptly found two relatively new YouTube uploads documenting machinists attempting to do something similar, though with these projects they just 3d printed the shells of the devices without any sort of internal lattice and then proceeded to insert a few steel rods, before filling them up with concrete.




Let's start the conceptual exploration by looking at existing FDM infill patterns and which of these, if any, could be used as is or can be adapted to be used directly as an infill to be poured over with, and embedded into concrete. Let's now proceed by taking inventory of already existing infill patterns.









Looking at all of the options presented in those videos, it would seem that only really the gyroid infill pattern would be suitable to use as is, since it can be filled with concrete all the way through whereas the other functional infill patterns mostly create little islands of disconnected voids that would thus each have to be filled individually. Other than that being difficult to practically do during the casting process, it would ultimately result in many islands of cured concrete that are not directly connected into one piece and as a result I believe it would lack the intended endurance. When such an object fails, I think it would crack and snap straight down through the plastic walls.




Infill patterns such as rectilinear and 3D honeycomb do have alternating layers, giving them one-layer-thickness-wide gaps between adjacent extrusions throughout. However given that a typical layer height is something between 0.1 mm to 0.5 mm, these would likely present too narrow a gaps for the concrete to effectively fill.





To give an illustration of the kind of idea that I'm talking about, the following video demonstrates a vase where the usual outer walls are removed and the whole vase is printed only as an infill pattern. He has added ribs which may or may not be necessary. My proposed method would be to take that skeletal infill and then print matching mold parts alongside it, so that it can sit inside of the mold and then be covered entirely by concrete, through and through, during the casting process.



The gyroid infill pattern has it's own drawbacks though as a structure intended to be filled and encased in some medium because of all of it's multidirectional overhanging arches. It just intuitively looks like a nightmare to get all of the air bubbles out of, and judging by the following video, that does indeed seem to be the case.



Concrete, no matter how fine a mixture, will necessarily be much coarser than resin goop and in light of that a vibratory surface may prove crucial. Luckily these can be made cheaply in a DIY fashion if necessary. It might be possible to combine a vibratory surface with a vacuum chamber into one machine; one inside of the other or one on top of the other.







Getting back to infill patterns, one relatively simple tweak that we could make on the slicer level would be to take the rectilinear infill algorithm and to modify it such that it only alternates its direction every few layers instead of every layer, thus stacking a few layers on top of each other before switching direction. This would produce beams that are elongated vertically, giving them a much more spacious arrangement.

These are the best pictures that I could find to give something of a visual representation:





The extruded infill walls can also be doubled up in thickness to two adjacent lines.
Again, this is the best example that I could found, it is a picture of a concrete 3d printer though of course I am talking about desktop FDM printers. I have to work with what I can get.



So to formulate some loose numbers, if we wanted the minimum sized opening to be something like 4 mm x 4 mm, then we would space the infill walls to be 4 mm apart and then have the printer extrude the rectilinear pattern in one direction for about ten layers at a time, at 0.4 mm layer height, stacking the lines directly on top of each other before switching direction and doing the next ten layers perpendicular to the previous ten, as per the rectilinear pattern.

For these types of infill applications, where the outer detail isn't even relevant, it might be worthwhile to use a larger nozzle opening, going from 0.4 mm diameter up to 0.6 mm or more. This would result in thicker walls without the need to double-up and it can also increase the layer-height thus reducing the total number of layers. It may even result in a stronger final object.





A different approach for a casting-appropriate infill might be to edit the grid, triangular or honeycomb patterns so that they would now include holes along their infill walls such that all the different cells are connected together as a singular space. To be filled.

The following two pictures serve to illustrate how a lone column in the grid infill would be thus riddled with holes. Imagine the metal pipe standing upright.




Here it might be particularly appropriate to have double-walls so that the printhead can go around 180° when printing the edges of the holes, which bear in mind stand vertical.




Larger grid unit sizes might be required by this method than with the previously described "elongated rectilinear" infill pattern. If these holes are to be sized at say 4 mm diameter, and as a side note the holes can be made to be square as well, then that might require the grid unit size for the infill grid to be up to 8 mm.



This same strategy of simply adding holes might be applicable to the cubic and the gyroid infill patterns as well, although their overhangs are a cause for concern and therefore optimal print settings would need to be established.






These regularly spaced holes, particularly as used in conjunction with the grid infill, could also serve as tunnels for the purpose of inserting metal rods into the form before casting, in order to further increase the strength of the material. Further plastic geometry can be added as tunnels for vertical rods.





This novel "punched grid" infill pattern could perhaps lend itself as an appropriate infill for more bulky parts that are to be cast in concrete. Such as this lathe headstock. Although I'm not sure if an embedded plastic grid would add to its rigidity.





For smaller parts these patterns that have so far been discussed may prove too large in terms of their minimum functional grid unit sizes to be able to be used effectively. One would want, after all, I would say at least four or more grid units spanning any particular cross-section of the final cast object in order to distribute the load evenly throughout the material, and to sufficiently reinforce those narrow and weak areas.

A minimum grid unit size of 8 mm and up, thus ideally 3.2 cm or more for the smallest possible cross-section; puts a constraint on the types of parts that one could conceivably reinforce with such infill methods. They would be usable only with more bulky frames or parts.

Now, other lesser known infill patterns do exist which may prove useful, such as the 3D diamond infill, but very little information on these exist online that I could find easily and so I will ignore them for the time being.




I should perhaps note that I am not a qualified engineer and that any parts produced by any of these methods would first need to be stringently tested using the proper methodologies before being relied upon.

One may, for example, wish to fabricate a machine such as this treadle lathe using our 3d-print-reinforced concrete method. One problem that immediately becomes apparent is that if you swapped the volume of wood used there for the same volume of concrete then that machine would weigh an absolute ton, and the functioning of any moving parts would be affected.
Because concrete is so damn heavy; if it could be sufficiently reinforced to be used as machine frames and other parts, it would best be used sparingly in the same manner as how thin metal rods would be used in the place of much thicker wooden beams. This would depend on the reinforced concrete's overall strength and rigidity.
