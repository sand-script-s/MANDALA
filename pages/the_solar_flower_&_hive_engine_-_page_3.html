Turning our attention now towards the engine units, let's analyze their design critically and look for any improvements that might be made.
If we could bump up the output of a single unit to say five watts or more, then spinning up a hundred of them could actually become a worthwhile endeavour.

As a refresher, here is yet another animation on the mechanistic principles of the basic Stirling engine.



So taking then that Gamma type Stirling Engine in the video above as our starting point, a few things start to pop out to me as perhaps needing to be looked into for possible revision.

To start with, it has a flat metal plate as the heating plate. Now a perfectly flat plane is obviously the least ammount of surface area that could cover that stretch of space. And to maximise heat transfer, really what you want is to maximise the surface area of the metal exposed to the air, in order to facilitate that.
So a flat heated plate might be alright to fry your onions on for breakfast; but if the goal is to heat up a volume of air as fast as you possibly can then it's a different matter.

What you want is a heating/cooling chamber. And then to fill that chamber with as much exposed metal as possible in some fashion such as stuffing it full of aluminium wool.



Aluminium conducts heat well, resists corrosion, and is soft enough of a metal that it can still be effectively worked on without the need of power tools if the situation demanded it.



Such an aluminium box can then be surrounded by copper pipes running hot water, and pressed firmly up against it, 'round the outside. The inside of the chamber can be stuffed full of aluminium wool as the cheapest solution. Other options include rows of metal pegs or strips welded or fixed in some other manner onto every side, lining the inside of the box and filling up the whole chamber. Similar in appearance I guess to some medieval torture devices.





The absolute best option would probably be to cut some pyramidal form of aluminium fins to be attached to each of the sides, such that when the box is closed the volume of the box is shared equally among all of the sides' fins. Perhaps some interlocking pattern. It may be beneficial to cut each of the container's sides with the fins already attached from a single block of aluminium, if attaching the fins to the sides of the box effectively turns out to be too much of a fuss.



Seeing as heat exchange is the backbone of the entire engine, one may as well go to town with it in the form of fractal tree heat exchange fins or some other exotic configuration which falls within one's manufacturing capabilities.

Going back for a moment to that wire EDM machine project named The Powercore. The reason I called it openish source is because even though it is technically open source, in that you can find the source code, I don't actually consider assets licenced under a Creative Commons NonCommercial license to be truly open source. And the Open Source Initiative seems to agree with this stance by not listing that license under their OSI approved open source licenses.



The Creative Commons suite of licenses work better really as licenses for creative assets, such as photos for example, rather than for programmatic source code for which a variety of other licenses have been specifically developed.
The uncertainty is further exacerbated when the license is extended to plans and schematics for hardware as this again leaves a lot of ambiguity as to what exactly it allows, and doesn't. For this reason yet again licenses have been developed specifically to address the issues related to open sourcing hardware designs and schematics.

If I use this wire EDM machine for example to manufacture components for a device that I intend to sell, is that allowed with this license or not? Furthermore if I use this wire EDM machine to produce parts with; which I then use in the process of building a completely different machine that then in turn fabricates components that I build into devices to sell, is that allowed or not?
Non-commercial licenses, seeming so divorced from everyday life, as well as ambigious enough to be unsettling, don't interest me all that much.

If one was bold one could interpret the license in their own favour and move forward. Though it may be safer in the long run to just contact the licensor, Rack Robotics in this case, and ask them what they had in mind when they applied that specific license, and to ask them to clarify their stance on your intended usecase.

Given that they sell kits for their projects, it may even be the case that all of the components in the project aren't readily available and that some are assumed to come purchased with the kit. I couldn't say as I haven't really looked into it all that much as of yet.



With all the kerfuffle surrounding the license though it may be better in the long run to design another one if that falls within one's capabilities, so as to not be beholden to the unclear licence or any unintended breach thereof.

Getting back to our modified Stirling engine, so we have decided to exchange the heating plate with a heating chamber, are we done? Not quite. See by doing that we have now compromised the functioning of the displacer piston.
It is the displacer piston's job to shield the heating and cooling plates, in turn, from coming into contact with many of the air particles and imparting their heat into them. This effectively 'tuns off' or at least lowers the influence of one of the heating-and-cooling plates at a time. It does this by physicaly shunting the air away from the plate and itself taking up that space, coming to a halt just before bumping into the plate. That's important by the way because otherwise it would create a little bit of a vacuum suction between itself and the heatplate, thus taking more energy to release and so impeding or even halting the whole system.
A displacer piston is unlike your usual compustion piston in the sense that it has a very large gap between the piston head and it's cylinder housing.
It's not meant to make any sort of seal, in fact it needs to be able to have quite a lot of air rush by it when it's pressing that air out of whichever side of the piston.
The case with our modified Stirling engine now is however so that by having replaced the heating-plate with a heating-chamber filled with metal structures, the displacer piston can no longer come in and physically occupy that space so as to force the air away from the heating elements. And as just discussed the displacer piston doesn't actually form a seal either, so hot air would just continue to form and rush past the piston into the main chamber from the heating chamber's side. This would break the basic functioning of the engine.

But when you think about it, the displacer piston concept itself seems perhaps a little janky. If its function is to shuffle air away from, and shield, a heating surface, then it can really only do it's job properly for a fraction of the cycle before it has to start scooting off towards the other side where it can again do its job for only a fraction of the cycle. It spends most of it's time in transit between two ideal states. This makes the machines hyper-ventilate rather than breathing deeply.

And then the whole idea of shuffling air around with a giant sponge or something flopping up and down. Doesn't it just seem like it's taking up a lot of energy trying to physically push the air around whilst also lugging the piston, whatever it ends up being made of, along to accomplish this. It really puts the stir in Stirling.
But air is perfectly capable of moving on it's own.
What if we replaced the displacer piston with two gates or valves, one at the mouth of each of the two heating/cooling chambers?
That could entail a lot less motion.

It will also enable the engine to function in a more binary manner. The gate to the hot chamber is fully opened at the same time that the gate to the cold chamber is fully closed, and then after half-a-cycle period it quickly flips to the hot chamber being fully closed and simultaneously the cold chamber fully opened.

So really what I'm talking about is a Stirless Engine.
